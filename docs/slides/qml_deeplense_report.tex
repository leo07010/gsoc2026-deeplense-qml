\begin{frame}{The problem \& the target}
\textbf{Task.} Classify strong-lensing images by dark-matter scenario:
\textcolor{q}{axion} (vortex) / \textbf{cdm} (subhalos) / \textbf{no\_sub} (smooth).
\medskip

\textbf{Classical SOTA.} MAE-pretrained ViT — \alert{AUC 0.968} (arXiv:2512.06642).
The encoder is fine-tuned end-to-end; the head is a single \texttt{Linear(192$\to$3)}.
\medskip

\textbf{Question.} Does attaching / embedding a \textcolor{q}{quantum} component
beat the \emph{same architecture without the circuit}?
\medskip

\begin{alertblock}{The one rule}
Every quantum model is paired with a \textbf{capacity-matched classical ``sham''}
(circuit $\to$ classical layer, matched dimensionality).
\textbf{quantum $-$ sham} is the only honest test.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Why it must tie — a certificate, not a guess}
\textbf{Geometric difference} $g(K_C, K_Q)$ (Huang et al., Nat.\ Commun.\ 2021):
if $g \ll \sqrt{N}$, \alert{no} quantum-kernel advantage is possible for \emph{any}
labels.
\medskip

\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
Kernel & 8 qubits & 12 qubits & 16 qubits \\
\midrule
Fidelity $g_{\min}$ & 5.7 & 2.7 & 2.3 \;(shrinks!) \\
Projected $g_{\min}$ & 11.5 & 12.3 & 10.1 \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{4}{c}{advantage threshold $2\sqrt{N}\approx \mathbf{57}$ — never approached} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\medskip

Adding qubits makes the fidelity kernel \emph{worse} (exponential concentration,
Thanasilp 2024). \textbf{The data is classically representable — by theorem.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The one thing that moved: symmetry $\neq$ quantum}
\textbf{Equivariant 2$\times$2} (C4 rotation invariance, low data):
\medskip

\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
\toprule
N/class & q-equiv & c-equiv & q-plain & c-plain \\
\midrule
50 & 0.652 & 0.656 & 0.525 & 0.554 \\
100 & 0.781 & 0.782 & 0.583 & 0.619 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\medskip

\begin{itemize}
\item \textcolor{good}{Equivariance is a large low-data win: $+0.11$ to $+0.18$.}
\item But \alert{q-equiv $\approx$ c-equiv}: the win is the \textbf{symmetry}, not
the circuit. A classical equivariant layer of matched size does the same.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Conclusion}
\begin{alertblock}{Headline}
On classical-simulator strong-lensing images, at $\le 16$ qubits, with
capacity-matched controls and a verified-trained circuit, \textbf{no quantum
advantage exists} — and a certificate ($g \ll \sqrt N$) explains \emph{why}.
\end{alertblock}
\medskip

\begin{itemize}
\item Reproduces the largest rigorous QML benchmarks (Bowles 2024; Schnabel 2025,
20\,000 models) — \textbf{plus} a certificate and an 8-battlefield map.
\item The ``successful'' QML papers win via engineered/quantum-native data or
\textbf{missing capacity-matched controls} — our sham would flag them.
\item \textbf{Contribution:} the first certificate-backed, sham-controlled QML
study on a real scientific benchmark. A rigorous null \emph{is} the result.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
